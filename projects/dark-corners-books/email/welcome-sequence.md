## Email 2: The Author's Story
**Send:** Day 2 (24 hours after email 1)
**Subject:** Why I write horror (it's not what you think)

```
[Header: Dark Corners Books — The Vault]

Rajesh,

People ask me why I write horror. They expect some dark answer — childhood
trauma, fascination with death, a goth phase that never ended.

The truth is simpler.

I write horror because it's the only genre where you can tell the truth.

In a romance novel, love conquers all. In a thriller, the detective solves
the case. In literary fiction, people grow and heal and find meaning.

Horror admits what every other genre pretends isn't true: sometimes things
don't work out. Sometimes the thing in the dark wins. Sometimes you're not
the protagonist — you're just in the wrong place at the wrong time, and the
universe doesn't care.

That honesty is why horror readers are different. We don't read for comfort.
We read to feel something real. To practice being afraid in a safe space.
To remind ourselves that the dark exists — and we're still here.

---

My grandmother in Kerala used to tell me stories about the *Yakshi* — a
female spirit that lured men into the forest. She didn't tell them to scare
me. She told them because they were true to her. The stories carried warnings,
cultural memory, a map of things to fear and avoid.

That's what I try to do. Write stories that feel true. That carry something
worth remembering. That make you check the locks — not because there's a
monster, but because you're not sure there isn't one.

---

Three books that shaped how I write horror:

🔮 **Thomas Ligotti — Teatro Grottesco**
   The philosopher of cosmic horror. Ligotti doesn't describe monsters.
   He describes the moment you realize there's no one coming to save you.

🌲 **Laird Barron — The Imago Sequence**
   Hard-boiled cosmic horror. Barron writes like a noir detective who
   stumbled into a Lovecraft story and decided to fight back.

🕯️ **T. Kingfisher — The Hollow Places**
   Folk horror with humor. Proof that horror can be terrifying and
   genuinely funny in the same paragraph.

## Email 3: Behind the Scenes (Social Proof + Craft)
**Send:** Day 3 (48 hours after email 2)
**Subject:** The scene that scared ME while I wrote it

```
[Header: Dark Corners Books — The Vault]

Rajesh,

When I was writing The Well Remembers, there's a scene in chapter 14 where
the protagonist, Arjun, descends into the well for the first time.

I had to stop writing twice.

The scene is simple: a man climbs down a rope ladder into a 200-year-old
well. The walls are wet. The air gets colder. His flashlight catches
something carved into the stone — a language he doesn't recognize but
somehow understands.

I knew what was at the bottom of the well. I'd plotted it. But writing the
descent — the slow, deliberate pace of it — hit differently than planning it.

Here's the technique I used (and you can steal this if you write):

**The Countdown Method**

Each paragraph counts down the distance to the bottom:
- "Forty feet above the water. The rope felt steady."
- "Thirty feet. The carvings were older here, almost worn smooth."
- "Twenty feet. The air tasted like copper and rot."
- "Ten feet. He heard breathing. It wasn't his."
- "Five feet. The flashlight died."

The reader knows something is at the bottom. Counting down makes every foot
unbearable. They can't skip ahead. They have to descend with him.

---

That's the kind of craft detail I think about obsessively. Horror isn't
about what's in the dark — it's about the walk toward it.
